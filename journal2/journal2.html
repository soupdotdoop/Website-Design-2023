Who is Frank Chimero?

Frank Chimero is a New York City based designer whose work has been featured in The Atlantic, T: The New York Times Style Magazine, Time, Slate, Monocle, and adopted by The New Yorker.

Chimero is also the author, designer, and illustrator of The Shape of Design, a philosophical handbook about design and making things for other people.

My Response

“Apple’s pursuit of cool yielded an incredibly fragile, willfully esoteric website that’s good for no one.”

I overall liked this reading and its approach to website design, but it also felt a little unfocused to me. A lot is being talked about, and I'm not sure if they all quite come together for me. The moment I started to feel lost is when Chimero started to discuss Hockney. When he started his arguments on “Edgelessness,” I started to understand it more, but I did not quite understand how it fit into his arguments on leaning into the natural structure of website tools. The edgelessness portion felt a little contradictory to his initial natural design arguments.


The reading had a mix of practical and more ideological/philosophical ideas. His arguments on “edgelessness” seemed like good practical advice to me. By not defining the edge, elements can appear more responsive.

“Apple’s pursuit of cool yielded an incredibly fragile, willfully esoteric website that’s good for no one.”

I thought her arguments on the naturality of website design to be really interesting. His main point being, website design is based on what HTML and CSS are inclined to do – “Many sites will share design solutions because we’re using the same materials” – and I liked that he encouraged this. Fighting against this is like fighting against nature. The example of a bear on a unicycle he used was perfect for understanding this.
